# Student edition

The student edition is the reader-facing PDF. Each lesson begins with a compact text: a monologue, dialogue, vocabulary study or an exercise. The topic may be cultural but the real subject is classroom language.

## voices after the lesson

The voices are recurring ways of noticing language.

Ines listens for phrasework that sounds alive. **Camila** reads the past like an archive. **Piotr** looks for argumentative bridges. **Rafa** asks whether anybody would say the sentence after missing a bus. Other personas appear when the lesson needs their particular kind of trouble.

## moods

Moods travel lighter. Nobody knows where they live and nobody expects them to return next week. They appear, react, disappear again, leaving behind a brief emotional reading: amused, analytical, bored, confused, impressed, irritated, sad or twisted.

An **irritated** mood may reduce a grand idea to something trivial. Another may notice the **sad** part before anyone else wants to admit it. **Twisted** usually finds and opens the basement door.

## class chronicle

At the end of each lesson package, the class chronicle records what the broader cast made of the lesson. It is part summary, part commentary, part small institutional rumour. By now the classroom has developed a memory of its own. The same voices keep returning, carrying their obsessions like suspicious luggage. The chronicle merely writes down what everybody pretends happened by accident.

## class cards

Every recurring voice has its own reference card, presented as the first appendix.

The card explains what that persona notices, what tends to irritate them, and why they keep returning to the same linguistic habits. Students gradually learn the cast just as they would recognise recurring columnists in a newspaper.

<figure markdown="span">
  ![Persona cards from Japan 1](assets/screens/japan1-persona-cards.png)
  <figcaption>Some people collect stamps. Topicalia collects editorial personalities.</figcaption>
</figure>

The cards are not required reading before the lesson. They quietly become useful after two or three encounters, when students begin to predict who will approve of a sentence and who will immediately start rewriting it.

## why appendices matter

Topicalia does not try to become a grammar reference. Instead, it indexes what appears inside the edition. If a connector, idiom or grammar pattern is marked in context, it can later appear in an appendix. The appendix is therefore a map of what a particular edition actually used. When the dust settles, the appendices simply reveal where the language has been.
